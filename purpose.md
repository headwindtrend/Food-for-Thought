# Lifetime is finite

But that's okay if you've lived it on purpose.

## The Cage You Don’t See

Most people wake up to an alarm clock.

They wash up, rush out the door, and spend the day earning money.

Later they spend that money—food, rent, entertainment, small comforts meant to make the day feel worthwhile.

Then they sleep.

And repeat.

No one announces it, but an unsettling question quietly hangs over this pattern:

**Is this really the purpose of a human life?**

Almost everyone would immediately say **no**.

Ask someone, “Is the purpose of your life to earn and spend?”

They’ll laugh.

“Of course not.”

Yet if you look at where most of their waking hours go—year after year—the answer seems uncomfortably close to **yes**.

## The Invisible Transformation

Something strange happens to people as they grow older.

When they are children, they ask enormous questions.

Why are we here?  
What is the world?  
What should I become?

But somewhere along the way those questions are quietly replaced.

Instead of asking **“What kind of life should I live?”**, people start asking:

- “What job should I get?”
- “How much should I earn?”
- “When should I retire?”

Without noticing it, the question shrinks.

A human life—vast, mysterious, and unrepeatable—gets compressed into a career path and a financial plan.

People begin introducing themselves as if they are job titles wearing human bodies.

“I’m a lawyer.”  
“I’m an engineer.”  
“I’m a manager.”  
“I’m a founder.”

A job is something you **do**.

Not something you **are**.

These are not identities.

They are **roles**.

And roles can quietly become cages.

Imagine introducing yourself this way:

“I’m someone who spends most of my waking life performing tasks for an organization.”

It suddenly sounds less glamorous.

## The Comfort of Avoidance

Why does this happen?

Because the real question is terrifying.

“What is the purpose of my life?”

There is no instruction manual.

No teacher hands out the answer.

No exam guarantees you did it correctly.

So people do something understandable: they **avoid the question**.

Career becomes a convenient anchor.

It provides structure, recognition, and social approval. It answers uncomfortable silence with something simple:

“I’m busy.”

But being busy is not the same as being purposeful.

A person can be extremely busy for forty years and still arrive at the end wondering:

**“What was all that for?”**

## The Most Expensive Illusion

Time feels abundant when you're young.

There will always be more years, more chances, more opportunities to think about the deeper things later.

But later has a strange habit of arriving suddenly.

One day you notice:

Your parents are older.

Your body feels different.

Half your life might already be gone.

The clock was ticking the entire time.

Quietly.

Mercilessly.

No negotiation.

No refunds.

No extensions.

## The Dangerous Lie

Society tells a subtle lie:

“Focus on earning first. Meaning can come later.”

But “later” often becomes **never**.

Not because people are lazy.

Because habits become stronger every year.

Avoidance becomes comfortable.

The questions become easier not to ask.

Until one day the opportunity to live differently simply disappears.

## A Simple but Difficult Step

Waking up does not require quitting your job or abandoning responsibilities.

It requires something much smaller—and much harder.

**Asking the question seriously.**

Not once.

Not as a passing thought.

But repeatedly.

What actually matters to me?

What kind of life would make me proud at the end?

What do I want my time on this planet to stand for?

No one else can answer it.

Not your employer.

Not your friends.

Not society.

Only you.

## The Courage to Look

Many people avoid this question because they fear the answer might disrupt their lives.

It might.

But ignoring it guarantees something worse.

Living someone else’s script.

There is a strange tragedy in human life:

People who are capable of immense creativity, curiosity, and meaning slowly reduce themselves to efficient routines.

Not because they had to.

Because they never paused long enough to ask whether they should.

## The Clock Is Already Running

You do not have unlimited time.

None of us do.

Every year is a page removed from the book.

Whether you choose to read it carefully or let it flip by unnoticed—that part is up to you.

So ask yourself honestly:

Are you living as a human being…

Or as a very well-trained **earning-and-spending machine**?

And if the answer makes you uncomfortable,

that discomfort might be the most important signal you receive today.

Because it means you’re still awake.

And as long as you're awake,

you still have time to choose differently.
