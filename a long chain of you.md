Elias had become very good at looking normal.

He still answered messages with “haha,” still showed up to work on time, still nodded when people asked, “You good?” as if the question were a formality and the answer had already been decided for him.

Inside, however, he had been quietly dismantling his life.

Not dramatically. Not in ways anyone would notice.

He stopped buying things for “later.”  
He let his plants die.  
He unsubscribed from long-term plans.  
He looked at his apartment not as a home, but as a room he was preparing to vacate.

He had not chosen a date exactly. But the thought had stopped being a fear and started becoming a kind of private furniture—always there, familiar, almost comforting.

It was strange, he thought, how calm despair could become.

His best friend, Daniel, noticed none of it at first.

Daniel was loud where Elias was quiet, impulsive where Elias was careful. They had known each other since university, long enough that silence between them never felt awkward.

One Thursday evening, Daniel came over uninvited, carrying cheap takeout and the confidence of a man who believed friendship exempted him from knocking.

“You look terrible,” Daniel said.

“Good to see you too.”

“I bring noodles and brutal honesty.”

They ate on the floor because Elias still hadn’t fixed the dining chair he’d broken months ago.

Most of the conversation was ordinary. Work complaints. Rent. A mutual friend’s terrible engagement photos.

Then Daniel stood to throw away the containers and noticed the folder on the counter.

Inside were documents too organized to be casual—bank information, passwords, insurance details, labeled carefully.

Life arranged into transferable pieces.

Daniel didn’t touch them.

He just stood there for a second too long.

When he turned around, Elias already knew.

Neither of them spoke.

The room changed shape.

Daniel came back and sat down slowly, like approaching an injured animal.

“How long?” he asked.

Elias stared at the floor.

“A while.”

Daniel nodded once.

Not angry. Not shocked. Just there.

“I kept thinking,” Elias said eventually, “if I made everything neat enough, maybe it would hurt less for everyone.”

Daniel leaned back against the couch.

“You were planning your own administrative death.”

Elias gave a dry laugh.

“That sounds about right.”

Another silence.

Then Elias said, very quietly, “I’m tired, Dan. I’m not having a dramatic episode. I’m just… tired. I feel like I’ve reached the end of what I can carry. And everyone keeps talking like things magically change if you wait long enough, but what if they don’t?”

Daniel listened.

No interruptions. No heroic speeches. Just listening.

The apartment was quiet except for the low hum of the refrigerator.

Elias sat on the floor, back against the couch, exhausted from crying and embarrassed by it. Daniel sat nearby, elbows on his knees, staring at nothing for a while.

Then, unexpectedly, Daniel said,

“Do you remember Veronica?”

Elias rubbed his face.

“Your ex? Back in college?”

Daniel gave a small nod.

“Yeah. I miss her. I miss her so much.”

Elias looked at him, surprised. Daniel rarely talked about Veronica. In fact, he almost never talked about anyone seriously.

“Is that why you never really had a long relationship after that?”

Daniel let out a quiet laugh.

“Probably.”

He paused.

“You know what the worst part is? I still don’t fully know why we ended.”

Elias frowned.

“What happened?”

Daniel leaned his head back against the couch.

“I wish I knew. I wish I hadn’t acted like it didn’t matter. I wish I had asked properly—really asked. Maybe she just wanted me to listen more. Maybe she needed me to take her seriously. Maybe I was too proud. Too stupid. Too young.”

He smiled, but there was no joy in it.

“I don’t know. I really don’t. And now it’s too late to do anything about it.”

Silence settled again.

Then Daniel turned slightly.

“Have you ever had that feeling? Wishing you hadn’t done something… or wishing you had done it differently?”

Elias gave a tired smile.

“Surely. Who hasn’t?”

Daniel nodded.

“That’s the strange thing about getting older. We look back and laugh at ourselves for stupid decisions. Or we sigh over people we hurt. Or opportunities we wasted.”

His voice grew quieter.

“Because the present us thinks, ‘I could have handled that better. If it were me now, I’d know what to do.’”

Elias stared at the floor.

Daniel continued.

“And usually, we’re right. We would do better now. Because we changed. Because we learned. Because we became someone else.”

He let that sit for a moment.

“Which is why I think suicide is such a dangerous decision.”

Elias looked up.

Daniel met his eyes.

“Because it lets one version of you—usually the most exhausted, most hurt, most cornered version—make the final decision for all the others.”

He gestured lightly between them.

“Nineteen-year-old me thought losing Veronica was just something to survive. Thirty-year-old me knows it shaped me. Maybe if nineteen-year-old me had been wiser, I’d have fought harder. Asked better questions. Loved better.”

He shook his head.

“But I only know that because I got older.”

His voice softened.

“And if twenty-five-year-old you decides life ends here, then forty-year-old you never gets to argue. Fifty-year-old you never gets to say, ‘Wait, you were wrong. We made it. We found something worth staying for.’”

Elias swallowed hard.

Daniel leaned forward.

**“We are not one person, Eli. We are a long chain of people, each one inheriting the consequences of the last.”**

He glanced at the folder still sitting on the counter.

“If one younger self gives up too early, all the future selves are erased before they can even speak.”

The room felt smaller somehow.

Elias whispered,

“And what if those future selves are just wishful thinking?”

Daniel answered without hesitation.

“Then let them prove it.”

A faint, broken laugh escaped Elias.

Daniel smiled a little.

“Postpone the execution. Allow an appeal. Very boring legal process.”

Despite himself, Elias laughed too.

Daniel’s expression turned serious again.

“Don’t let pain become a dictator. It always demands immediate power. It always says it should decide for everyone.”

He reached over and tapped Elias once on the shoulder.

“But pain is temporary. Death isn’t.”

Elias looked down, eyes burning again.

“I don’t know how to keep going.”

Daniel’s answer came gently, but firm.

“Then don’t keep going alone.”

And for the first time in a long time, Elias believed that maybe the story of his life had not reached its ending—

only a chapter that hurt too much to read clearly.
